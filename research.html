Our Approach

How Do We Approach Those Problems?

Approach: Combining behavioral science and computer science to build passive sensors and data analytics to help us Understand, Sense, Detect, Predict, and ultimately Prevent problematic behavior. Below we provide an on-going research related to overeating.

Understand: Positive correlation between caloric intake and eating behaviors

Why: To understand what features characterize problematic behaviors and what are the antecedents and proximal determinants to problematic behaviors (like overeating).

What: Using surveys, focus groups, and human computer interaction focused studies we can learn how to optimize passive sensing systems.

Example: We recently we showed in a study that feeding gestures, eating duration, and # of swallows positively correlate with caloric intake. We also designed systems to detect them using wearables. But will anyone wear them? In a recent survey of 93 people we show that 88% said they would wear our wrist-worn sensor, and 62% said they would wear a personalized version of our neck-worn sensor.

Sense: Build mHealth Sensor systems to detect overeating and its contextual factors

Why: To help us detect the essential behavioral factors.

What: Build passive sensing systems comprising sensors, an information gateway (smartphone), and a backend database.

Example: Our solution involves a neck-worn sensor to detect behaviors related to swallowing and chewing, a wrist-worn sensor to detect feeding gestures, heart rate and activity, and a video camera to capture truth and a smartphone to capture everything else.

Detect: Applying bag-of-words to segment and detect feeding gestures

Why: To transform the sensor signals to meaningful detectable features.

What: Build human activity recognition frameworks and methods that improve our ability to detect important behavioral features.

Example: We design generalized and personalized machine learning models to detect low-level features like feeding gestures, swallows, and alone time.

Predict: Applying unsupervised clustering algorithms to determine behavioral phenotypes to overeating

Why: To understand which features are the most predictive of the behavioral phenotypes.

What: Build machine learning and mixed-effects based models to discover the behavioral phenotypes and factors that are most relevant.

Example: We are applying unsupervised machine learning algorithms to understand the most predictive behavioral features and in doing so validating known clinical behavioral phenotypes and discovering new ones. In one study we uncover positive and negative affect based overeating phenotypes.
